Chapter 01 — The National Picture

Progress is real. But 1 in 5 girls is still not enough.

India has made meaningful strides in reducing child marriage over three decades. The national rate has fallen from 49.4% in 1993 to 23.3% in 2021 — cutting the prevalence nearly in half. That represents millions of girls whose lives were not derailed by early marriage.

But the headline decline masks an uncomfortable truth: progress has slowed. Between NFHS-3 and NFHS-4 (2005–2016), the rate dropped 17.7 percentage points. Between NFHS-4 and NFHS-5 (2016–2021), it dropped just 3.5 points. The rate of reduction is decelerating — and the SDG 5.3 target of elimination by 2030 is not on track at current pace.

"One in five girls and nearly one in six boys are still married below the legal age of marriage in India."

Chapter 02 — The State Story

The national average obscures a crisis concentrated in a handful of states.

Child marriage in India is not evenly distributed. It is geographically concentrated — with a small number of states accounting for a disproportionate share of the national burden. And within those states, the rates are not just high — they are crisis-level.

West Bengal, Bihar, and Tripura each have child marriage rates above 40% — more than double the national average. Over half of all girls in India who were married as children live in just five states: Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, West Bengal, Maharashtra, and Madhya Pradesh. At the other end, states like Kerala, Himachal Pradesh, and Goa have rates below 10% — demonstrating that elimination is achievable.

The variation across states reveals something important for program design: child marriage is not a uniform national problem. It is a concentrated, structural problem in specific geographies — requiring targeted, locally-adapted responses rather than blanket national strategies.

Chapter 03 — The Drivers

Child marriage is a symptom. Poverty, education gaps, and gender norms are the disease.

Understanding why child marriage persists requires looking beyond the practice itself to the structural conditions that enable it. The NFHS-5 data reveals four interlocking drivers — each of which must be addressed for programs to achieve lasting impact.

📚

Girls' education

Each additional year of girls' education delays marriage by 0.36 years. Girls with no education are married as children at 5x the rate of educated girls.

63% of uneducated girls married before 18 (Bihar)

💰

Household poverty

Girls from the poorest wealth quintile are married as children at 6x the rate of girls from the wealthiest quintile in Bihar. Poverty drives marriage as economic strategy.

54% of poorest quintile girls married before 18

🏘️

Rural residence

Rural girls face significantly higher rates of child marriage than urban peers — 30% vs 15% nationally. Infrastructure, school access, and social norms all play a role.

Rural rate 2x higher than urban nationally

👥

Caste & social group

Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe communities face higher prevalence — 49% in Bihar — reflecting intersecting vulnerabilities of caste, poverty, and geographic isolation.

49% among SC/ST communities in Bihar (NFHS-5)

Chapter 04 — Program Implications

What the evidence says about what works — and where to invest.

The data from NFHS-5 is not just descriptive — it points toward a clear evidence base for program design. Organizations working to eliminate child marriage in India have a roadmap. The question is whether interventions are targeting the right levers, at the right scale, in the right geographies.

01

Prioritize girls' secondary education — especially in Bihar, West Bengal, UP, and Jharkhand

The education-marriage relationship is the strongest and most consistent finding across all NFHS rounds. Programs that keep girls in school past primary level have the highest evidence base for reducing child marriage.

Evidence: OR=7.5 for no education vs higher education (UNFPA Bihar analysis)

02

Integrate economic support for families — conditional cash transfers and livelihood programs

Where poverty is the primary driver of early marriage, education-only programs are insufficient. Haryana's Apni Beti, Apna Dhan conditional cash transfer program demonstrates that financial incentives can meaningfully delay marriage.

Evidence: Wealth quintile odds ratio 1.5x for poorest vs richest (NFHS-5)

03

Deploy concentrated resources in highest-burden states rather than uniform national programming

Over half of child brides in India live in five states. Resource allocation that mirrors this geographic concentration — rather than distributing evenly — will generate significantly greater impact per dollar invested.

Evidence: 5 states account for >50% of all child marriages nationally (UNICEF 2023)

04

Engage communities, religious leaders, and families — not just girls

Child marriage is sustained by community norms and family decision-making. Programs that work only with girls miss the gatekeepers. Community-level norm change, including male and elder engagement, is essential for durable progress.

Evidence: Rural social norms cited as key driver across multiple NFHS rounds

Chapter 05 — The 2030 Question

At the current pace, India will not meet its SDG 5.3 commitment.

The Sustainable Development Goals call for the elimination of child marriage globally by 2030. India has committed to this target. But the math is unforgiving.

Between 2016 and 2021, India's child marriage rate fell by just 3.5 percentage points — from 26.8% to 23.3%. To reach zero by 2030 requires a reduction of 23.3 percentage points in 9 years — more than 6x the pace of the last five years. Without significant acceleration in program investment, policy enforcement, and community-level change, the 2030 target will not be met.

This gap is not inevitable. States like Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh have demonstrated that rapid acceleration is possible — both registered reductions of nearly 10 percentage points between NFHS-4 and NFHS-5, compared to the national average of 3.5. The conditions for faster progress exist. What is needed is political will, sustained investment, and program models that address the structural drivers, not just the symptom.

SDG Target 5.3 — Eliminate child marriage by 2030

The data tells us where. Now programs need to act.

Child marriage is not a uniform national problem — it is geographically concentrated, structurally driven, and responsive to the right interventions. Organizations working in Bihar, West Bengal, Jharkhand, and Uttar Pradesh have the evidence base they need to design programs that work. The question is scale and urgency.
