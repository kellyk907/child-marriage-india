An independent public health research project examining the prevalence, drivers, and program implications of child marriage across India's states — using data from the National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5, 2019–21), UNICEF, UNFPA, and The Lancet Global Health.

🔗 india-child-marriage.netlify.app

## About This Project

This project takes a data storytelling approach to child marriage in India — presenting the evidence as a scrolling narrative that moves from national trends to state-level disparities to structural drivers to actionable program recommendations. It is designed to communicate complex public health data to nonprofit, missions, and policy audiences in a clear, compelling format.

The analysis applies a **program evaluation framework** to ask: where is progress happening, where is it stalling, and what does the evidence say about what works?

---

## The Story in Five Chapters

**Chapter 01 — The National Picture**
India has reduced child marriage from 49.4% (1993) to 23.3% (2021) — meaningful progress over three decades. But the pace of reduction has slowed dramatically: the rate dropped 17.7 points between 2005 and 2016, and only 3.5 points between 2016 and 2021.

**Chapter 02 — The State Story**
Child marriage is not evenly distributed. West Bengal (42%), Bihar (41%), and Tripura (40%) have rates more than double the national average. Over half of all child brides in India live in just five states. Meanwhile, Kerala (6%) and Himachal Pradesh (5%) demonstrate that near-elimination is achievable.

**Chapter 03 — The Drivers**
Four interlocking structural drivers sustain child marriage: girls' education gaps, household poverty, rural residence, and caste-based social norms. Girls with no education are married as children at 5x the rate of educated girls — making secondary education the single strongest protective factor.

**Chapter 04 — Program Implications**
The NFHS-5 data points to four evidence-based program priorities: prioritizing girls' secondary education in high-burden states, integrating economic support for families, concentrating resources geographically, and engaging communities and families — not just girls.

**Chapter 05 — The 2030 Question**
The SDG 5.3 target calls for elimination of child marriage by 2030. At the current pace of reduction, India is not on track — requiring 6x faster progress than the 2016–2021 period to meet the target.

- **Program recommendations** — four evidence-based interventions with citations

---

## Program Recommendations

1. **Prioritize girls' secondary education** in Bihar, West Bengal, UP, and Jharkhand — the education–marriage relationship is the strongest and most consistent finding across all NFHS rounds
2. **Integrate economic support for families** — conditional cash transfers and livelihood programs address poverty as a driver of marriage
3. **Concentrate resources geographically** — 5 states account for over 50% of all child marriages nationally
4. **Engage communities, families, and religious leaders** — norm change requires working with gatekeepers, not just girls
